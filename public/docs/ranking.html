Citation ranking

Search is useful in itself – but consider the graph of events for your keyword query a moment longer. It’s not a graph of all the events that match your search, but of the most highly cited ones. In general, these fall into two categories:

(1) Accurately reported events that are important enough to play a role in many different timelines. For example, the signing of the United States Declaration of Independence on August 2, 1776.

(2) Highly controversial misinterpretations of the facts, influential views that have since been superceded, and common misinformation. For example, that the United States declared independence from Britain on July 4, 1776.[*] As this example illustrates, in these cases even influential misinformation is a significant part of the whole story.

In other words, the graph shows events that would appear in a textbook or basic introduction on your topic, with some historiographical details as well.

[*] Actually, the documents make clear that Congress voted to declare independence on July 2, 1776 and signed the document on August 2, 1776.

Emergent properties

Certainly, one or two users might have created timelines that include blatantly wrong information – say, that the United States declared independence on July 14, 1789. However, as others construct their timelines, they’ll generally choose to use events that reflect their best judgment, so as not to discredit their own work.

So Emergent Time aggregates many users’ work on their independent timelines to form a shared timeline that reflects the community’s current overall understanding.

For most historical topics, this means the collective timeline for a given topic will show an accurate overview of major events. The more active the Emergent Time community is in a particular area, the more focused the overview timeline.

It’s an example of what scientists call an “emergent property.” Many individuals operate in an environment, forming complex and evocative patterns as a collective.

Behind the curtain

How does it all work? Emergent Time incorporates on the same link analysis algorithm as Google search (called PageRank).

But instead of using it to rank web pages, Emergent Time looks at people’s timelines, events, and comments. Using a highly-ranked event strengthens your timeline’s ranking; and conversely, events that play a role in an important timeline receive a boost too. This helps Emergent Time locate events that are highly important to the community, and foreground them.

It's a very different approach to digital humanities than past work on textual criticism or computational linguistics. Instead it focuses on collaboration and the practice of research. It finds value and knowledge embedded in the social relations between scholars, and works to bring that collective thought process to light.

N.B. Emergent Time has no affiliation with Google, nor does it use Google’s index service. It merely uses an algorithm similar to PageRank to analyze citations.
